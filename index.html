A diabetes diagnosis changes things. It can feel overwhelming at first — a lot of new information, new routines, new things to monitor and manage.

This page won't pretend that's easy. But it will tell you this: understanding your diabetes is one of the most powerful things you can do. The more you know, the more control you have.

Whether you were diagnosed recently or have been living with diabetes for years, there is always more available to you.

Has your GP told you your blood sugar is a bit high?

This is called pre-diabetes or non-diabetic hyperglycaemia. It means your blood sugar is above normal, but not yet in the diabetic range. It is not a diagnosis of diabetes — and it does not have to become one.

More than 6,000 people across Weymouth and Portland are in this position right now. Many don't know what to do with the information. This is what you can do:

  • Small changes to food and movement make a real difference at this stage
  • Losing 5 to 10% of body weight if you are overweight significantly reduces risk
  • You may be eligible for a free NHS programme specifically designed to help
  • Ask your GP or practice nurse whether you qualify for a referral

Step two

Manage it day to day

Diabetes management is not about perfection. It is about building habits that work for your life, with the right support around you.

Blood sugar

  • Know your target range
  • Understand what raises and lowers it
  • Keep a record to spot patterns
  • Talk to your practice nurse about monitoring

Food

  • No single diet works for everyone
  • Reducing refined carbohydrates helps most people
  • Small, sustainable changes beat big overhauls
  • Specialist dietitian support is available locally

Movement

  • Physical activity directly lowers blood sugar
  • Even a short walk after eating helps
  • Aim for something you will actually do
  • LiveWell Dorset can support you

Medication

  • Take it as prescribed, even on good days
  • Tell your team about any side effects
  • Medication and lifestyle work together
  • Your practice nurse is your first point of contact

Under 40 and living with diabetes?

The number of younger adults being diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes is rising. Developing it under 40 carries a higher risk of complications compared to later-life diagnosis — but it also means you have more time, and more opportunity, to make changes that matter. Your practice nurse can make sure you are getting the right support for your age and circumstances. You do not have to manage this alone.

Step three

Live well alongside it

Diabetes is a long-term condition. Living well with it is about the whole picture — not just blood sugar, but energy, mood, sleep, and connection.

Sleep

  • Poor sleep raises blood sugar — they are directly connected
  • Keep a consistent bedtime and wind-down routine
  • If sleep is a persistent problem, mention it to your practice nurse

Emotional wellbeing

  • Diabetes distress is real and very common
  • Feeling overwhelmed, frustrated or burnt out is a recognised part of living with a long-term condition
  • Steps 2 Wellbeing offers free NHS talking therapies — no GP referral needed

Remission

  • Type 2 diabetes remission is possible for some people
  • It means blood sugar levels return to a normal range without medication
  • The Path to Remission programme is available via NHS Dorset — ask your GP

When to contact your GP or seek urgent help

  • Blood sugar that will not come down despite your usual measures
  • Signs of a hypoglycaemic episode — shaking, sweating, confusion
  • New or worsening foot problems — do not wait
  • Changes in vision
  • Unexplained weight loss or fatigue
  • Diabetes that is stopping you coping day to day
  • If you are struggling emotionally, call NHS 111

Call 999 or go to A&E if someone is in immediate danger or you are very worried that waiting is not safe.
